## 🧭 How I think — five principles I work by

These are the principles that guide every risk assessment, gap analysis, and audit I touch. They're also why my work tends to land cleanly with both engineers and auditors.

<details>
<summary><strong>1. Risk Before Compliance</strong> — never start with a checklist</summary>

I don't start with "what does the standard say?" I start with *"what could go wrong, and how would it affect the business?"* When I assessed risks for a SaaS platform across 5 categories (Engineering, Operational, Vendor, Legal, Business), every security decision was driven by a scored risk — not by which rule came next in a standards document.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>2. Severity-Based Prioritization</strong> — not all gaps are equally urgent</summary>

When I found 6 types of compliance gaps across 12 frameworks, I classified each by severity — Critical, High, Medium, Low. A missing security safeguard protecting customer data is Critical. A missing documentation link is Low. Resources go where the risk is highest.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>3. Governance by Design</strong> — security built in, not bolted on</summary>

Security should be built into how teams work — not added after the fact. When I designed security checkpoints for a SaaS platform's development process, I placed compliance gates at every phase with 5 trigger types (data, privacy, security, regulatory, AI). Security gets checked during development, not discovered as a problem after release.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>4. Evidence-Driven, Not Opinion-Driven</strong> — proof beats claims</summary>

Every finding I raise is backed by proof. When tracking compliance across 434 controls, I score evidence on 4 dimensions — completeness, freshness, format, and coverage — with quality grades from A to F. If the evidence quality is poor, the control gets flagged regardless of what the responsible person claims. Audits should be objective, not based on assumptions.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>5. Translate Risk Into Business Language</strong> — leadership-ready, always</summary>

A risk list that leadership can't understand is a risk list that gets ignored. I translate technical security risks into business terms — potential revenue impact, regulatory fines, and customer trust. The risk heat maps I design aren't just for analysts — they give executives a quick view of where the organization stands, what's being addressed, and what needs urgent attention.

</details>
